The Stratigraphic Model

The First Part of Ieronimo, The Murder of John Brewen, and the Editorial Architecture of the Shakespearean Canon
Avishai Roif  ·  Independent Researcher
Method  Burrows Delta · Bayesian Attribution · N-gram · Welch's t-test Corpus  Kyd · Shakespeare · Marlowe Period  1592–1604 Venue  Academia.edu
Abstract

This article argues that The First Part of Ieronimo (1605) is a composite text exhibiting two statistically distinct authorial layers: a Kydian substrate and an editorial layer indistinguishable from the Shakespearean canon (Burrows Δ = 0.33; Cohen's d = 1.37; N-gram likelihood ratio ~337,000:1). The Murder of John Brewen (1592) is shown to encode Edward de Vere's private narrative of the Anne Cecil marriage crisis through deliberate perspectival inversion. Together, these findings reconstruct the political mechanism by which de Vere's systematic editorial transformation of Kyd's dramatic architecture produced the Shakespearean canon, and explain Kyd's destruction in 1593 as the consequence of unauthorized biographical disclosure.

Three Interlocking Claims

The Argument
I
The First Part of Ieronimo is a composite text. It exhibits two statistically distinct authorial layers: Layer C, a Kydian substrate of diplomatic and martial scenes, and Layer A, an editorial superstructure of gallery speeches, elegies, and soliloquies indistinguishable from the Shakespearean canon by Burrows Delta, Bayesian attribution, and N-gram analysis.
II
The Murder of John Brewen is a biographical cipher. The pamphlet does not encode the historical facts of the Brewen murder case but the subjective narrative of Edward de Vere concerning the breakdown of his marriage to Anne Cecil — rendered in deliberately inverted form, displacing guilt from husband to wife in a manner that requires intimate knowledge of de Vere's private self-understanding.
III
Kyd's destruction in 1593 was not incidental. The withdrawal of de Vere's patronage following Kyd's arrest was not a passive failure to protect a servant but an active response to the unauthorized encoding of de Vere's private narrative in a published pamphlet. The consolidation of the canon under the Shakespeare name follows directly from the removal of the collaboration's substrate author.

Key Result

The Marlowe control corpus yields Δ = 1.34 for Layer A — substantially more distant than the Shakespearean canon. This rules out the possibility that Layer A simply reflects any later Elizabethan hand. The signal is specifically Shakespearean, not generically non-Kydian.

Interpretation

Cohen's d = 1.37 is conventionally classified as a large effect and is substantially above the threshold at which single authorship can be maintained. The shift from coordinate to subordinate construction is not a matter of degree but of cognitive architecture — a fundamentally different model of how dramatic speech constructs argument and projects interiority.

The Murder of John Brewen — Seven Correspondences

The pamphlet encodes not the historical facts of the Brewen murder but de Vere's private self-narrative of the Anne Cecil crisis — the version that placed guilt entirely on his wife. This configuration is not derivable from the public record.

Perspectival Inversion Analysis — Seven Correspondences
1
Triangular configuration. Woman committed by family arrangement to a man she does not love, while privately attached to another. In Brewen: Anne Welles, John Brewen, John Parker. In the de Vere case: the Burghley-arranged marriage and the accusation against Sackville.
2
Material transaction as bond. Brewen bestows "gold and jewels" as courtship gifts; legal action for their recovery forces the reluctant marriage. De Vere's marriage was explicitly financial; his 1576 correspondence with Burghley is substantially concerned with debt and land sales.
3
Enforced separation and naming refusal. Anne Welles "lay not with him after the first night... neither could she abide to be called after his name." De Vere took rooms at Charing Cross, stipulated Anne not attempt to speak to him, and refused to acknowledge her as Countess of Oxford.
4
Disputed child born during husband's absence. In Brewen, the child's illegitimacy is confirmed. In the historical record, Elizabeth Vere was born during de Vere's Italian tour and the accusation was eventually withdrawn. Kyd encodes de Vere's private version — not what happened, but what de Vere claimed to believe.
5
Prior physical possession. Parker "had ere then lien with her and gotten her with child" before the marriage. De Vere's accusation against Sackville implied exactly this prior attachment.
6
Secular discovery mechanism. Discovery occurs through an overheard conversation, not divine providence — anomalous for Kyd, whose theological signature is "Murder will out." The secular mechanism is a structural necessity imposed by the biographical source: divine intervention would have implied de Vere was wrong.
7
Temporal distance. The murder occurred c.1589–90; Kyd published the pamphlet in August 1592 — nearly three years later. Kyd was not a journalist. He returned to a cold case for a purpose: encoding a private narrative in a form the patron would recognize and readers would not.
The Methodological Crux

A writer working from public knowledge could have produced a text where a husband wrongly accuses a wife — the historically accurate version. What Kyd produced is a text where the wife's guilt is confirmed. This cannot be derived from the public record. It corresponds specifically to de Vere's private self-narrative. Kyd's access to that narrative is explicable only on the hypothesis of direct household service — precisely the relationship his letter to Puckering attests.

1593 as Structural Turning Point

The arrest of Thomas Kyd in May 1593 has been understood as an incidental casualty of the Dutch Church Libel investigation. The present argument disputes the sufficiency of this explanation. Three features of the documentary record are inconsistent with it.

Three Anomalies in the Documentary Record
I
The unnamed patron. Kyd's letter to Puckering refers to "my Lord... whom I have served" without naming him. The patron's failure to intercede — despite having court connections — requires explanation. The present argument: de Vere had reason to wish Kyd removed from circulation.
II
The omission under interrogation. Kyd named Marlowe, Harriot, Warner, Roydon — but not de Vere, who was among the most prominent members of the circle. A man being tortured who omits the most powerful member of the group he is describing is a man who fears naming him more than not naming him.
III
The coroner's roll. Forbes's 1973 transcription of CLA/041/IQ/01/011 establishes that the Brewen inquest predates the surviving roll. Every account of the Brewen case derives ultimately from Kyd's pamphlet. The gap between the historical event and Kyd's account is precisely the space in which the biographical encoding operated.
Consequence

The name "William Shakespeare" first appears on published texts in 1593–94 — the years immediately following Kyd's destruction. The coincidence is not biographical accident. The editorial-compositional practice that had hitherto operated through the Kyd collaboration was now redirected toward other substrate materials and consolidated under a name that would become canonical.

The Jubilee Chronological Anchor

Layer A of Ieronimo contains an allusion to "the Iubile at Rome" as a recent event. The Roman Jubilee most plausibly referred to is that of 1600. Thomas Kyd died in August 1594. No mechanism exists by which Kyd could have written this allusion. Its presence in Layer A confirms that Layer A was composed or substantially revised after 1600 — that is, during the period when de Vere, who died in June 1604, was still living. This chronological anchor is independent of the computational analysis and provides a non-statistical constraint on the attribution.
